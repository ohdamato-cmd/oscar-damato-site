Cheniere Is Over-Earning at Peak.
The Market Is Paying as If Peak Is Permanent.

Cheniere Energy (LNG) is a Short — but not a collapse call. At ~$274/share, the stock prices in perpetual peak-contract economics at 11–12× forward EBITDA, a 22% premium to infrastructure peers. We are not underwriting an LNG demand collapse. We are underwriting a normalization of earnings and multiples as 40% more global supply arrives by 2028. We see ~60% downside to a 12-month price target of $110, with a bear case of $80.

Cheniere Energy is a Short — but the thesis requires precision. We are not calling an LNG demand collapse, a contract breakdown, or a balance-sheet crisis. We are calling a normalization of earnings and valuation multiples on a business the market is pricing as though today’s peak conditions are permanent. At ~$274/share, the stock embeds 11–12× EV/EBITDA on what is, by Cheniere’s own 2026 guidance, a $6.75–$7.25B EBITDA run rate. The question is not whether Cheniere earns that EBITDA today. It does. The question is whether the market should pay 11–12× for it, and whether that EBITDA is the right baseline to capitalize. We argue both answers are no, and the combined error implies ~60% downside.

The liquefaction infrastructure Cheniere built — what the industry simply calls “the machine” — is real. In February 2016, the first LNG tanker to depart the contiguous United States slipped out of Sabine Pass, Louisiana — an infrastructure bet that most of the energy establishment called delusional. Ten years later, the United States is the world’s largest LNG exporter. Cheniere exported a record 670 cargoes in 2025, generating $19.98 billion in revenue — up 27% from 2024’s $15.7 billion (Cheniere 10-K). Shares trade near $274, implying a market capitalization of approximately $58 billion and an enterprise value of $81–83 billion. The bull case is not absurd.

The mispricing has two distinct, addable components. First, the EBITDA baseline: the market is capitalizing near-peak earnings of ~$7B as a permanent run rate, ignoring that the 40% global supply addition arriving by 2028 will compress LNG spreads and, more critically, create a buyer’s market at the 2030s contract renewal cycle. A normalized EBITDA of $7.5–$8.5B — crediting partial Stage 3 volume growth but discounting renewal fee pressure from $2.50–$3.00 toward $1.75–$2.25/MMBtu — is a more defensible long-term baseline. Second, the multiple: at 11–12× EV/EBITDA, Cheniere trades at a 22% premium to comparable infrastructure peers (EPD, KMI, WMB at 9.2–10.1×). That premium is defensible only if the growth story holds. If fee compression materializes, a re-rate to 8–9× — still infrastructure-like, not distressed — compounds the downside.

The Toll Road Model — and Its Limits

Cheniere doesn’t own gas reserves. It charges a fixed liquefaction fee of $2.50–$3.00/MMBtu to convert feed gas into LNG regardless of commodity prices. This fee structure created the appearance of a utility-like business insulated from commodity volatility. Revenue = contracted volumes × liquefaction fee + variable margin on spot cargoes. EBITDA tracks capacity utilization and fee levels, not Henry Hub or the Title Transfer Facility (TTF), the Dutch benchmark for European gas prices. Over 90% of volumes are sold under long-term contracts averaging 15–20 years. The machine is built.

The model’s defining test came in 2022: Russia’s invasion of Ukraine shattered Europe’s energy architecture. TTF prices above $70/MMBtu drove Cheniere revenues to $33.8 billion in 2023. When the crisis premium faded, revenues fell to $15.7 billion in 2024 before recovering to $19.98 billion in 2025 on higher volumes. The toll road held — but the traffic proved cyclical. The structural question is what happens at the 2030s contract renewal cycle when counterparties face a very different supply market than the one that signed today’s contracts.

The Gap the Market Isn’t Pricing

Between 2024 and 2028, global nameplate LNG liquefaction capacity will increase by approximately 193 million tonnes per annum (mtpa) — equivalent to roughly 260 billion cubic meters (bcm) of gas — a 40% increase from the ~474 mtpa baseline, per IEEFA’s Global LNG Outlook 2024. The United States contributes ~45% of that increase; Qatar’s North Field expansion adds 88 bcm/y; Canada’s LNG Canada contributes 14 mtpa. According to the IEA World Energy Outlook 2024 (STEPS scenario), total LNG demand growth over the same period amounts to roughly 200 bcm — generating a structural surplus of approximately 65 bcm by 2030. Statista’s LNG trade volume projections corroborate this structural deceleration: global LNG trade is projected to grow at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of approximately 2.5% through 2030, compared to an 8.4% CAGR in 2020–2023 — a deceleration of more than two-thirds, driven primarily by European import substitution plateauing and Asian buyer optionality on renewable alternatives (Statista, Global LNG Trade Outlook, 2024).

In a surplus market, spot prices compress. More critically, counterparties approaching contract renewals in the mid-2030s will face a fundamentally different negotiating environment. Today’s $2.50–$3.00/MMBtu liquefaction fee is a seller’s market price. In a world with 40% more capacity than the market built in the preceding decade, $1.50–$2.00/MMBtu is a plausible buyer’s market outcome. That 30–40% compression is the fulcrum of the short thesis. PitchBook data shows capital flows into global LNG and natural gas infrastructure peaked in 2022–2023 and have declined markedly as energy transition capital has accelerated — a forward-looking signal that long-horizon buyers are repricing gas asset duration.

The Demand Side: Priced for Growth That May Not Arrive

The bull case rests on three pillars: European structural demand, Asian industrialization, and U.S. political tailwinds. Each is real. None is unconditional. According to the IEA World Energy Outlook 2024, European LNG imports are projected to peak near 148 mtpa around 2027 then decline as wind and solar scale past fossil fuels in EU electricity generation — which occurred for the first time in 2025. U.S. suppliers accounted for 77.5% of European LNG imports in 2025, up from 57.6% in 2024, per S&P Global Energy CERA (Cambridge Energy Research Associates) data. That share dominance is a feature of today’s tightness, not necessarily tomorrow’s surplus.

Asia is the stronger long-run case. India is projected to triple gas demand by 2040 per EIA International Energy Outlook data, and Southeast Asian industrialization represents the single largest potential source of incremental LNG demand globally. But Asia is also price-sensitive. China, the marginal buyer, is simultaneously the world’s largest solar installer and its most aggressive nuclear builder: as of early 2026, China has approximately 28 reactors under construction and 30+ planned, targeting 150 gigawatts (GW) of nuclear capacity by 2035 per China’s National Energy Administration — more reactors under construction than the rest of the world combined. LNG cannot displace Chinese coal at scale without prices near $3–5/MMBtu — barely above U.S. production cost plus liquefaction. The IEA projects Chinese LNG import growth decelerating sharply by the early 2030s as domestic renewables and nuclear displace gas-fired power generation. The CB Insights Energy Transition report (2024) documents accelerating corporate energy procurement shifts across Asian markets away from LNG toward renewables and storage.

What About AI & Data Centers?

The data center demand thesis deserves a direct answer, because it is the most credible near-term bull argument. U.S. artificial intelligence (AI) and hyperscale data center electricity demand is growing at an estimated 15–20% annually through 2030, per the International Energy Agency’s Electricity 2024 report. Natural gas-fired generation is the dominant bridge fuel for this load growth, as grid operators cannot balance intermittent renewable capacity fast enough to meet it. This is real, and it meaningfully supports domestic gas demand — which supports feedgas supply into Cheniere’s liquefaction trains.

The question is not whether this is true. It is whether it is already priced in. The data center/AI energy theme has been the dominant narrative in U.S. energy equities since late 2023; Cheniere’s 11–12× premium multiple almost certainly embeds this optionality. More specifically, the data center thesis supports near-term utilization — it does not resolve the contract renewal fee problem, which is the fulcrum of the short thesis. In 2032–2035, when Cheniere’s counterparties face renewal decisions, the question will be whether a structurally oversupplied global LNG market gives them leverage to renegotiate fee rates — regardless of how many data centers are running in Virginia. Data centers support the floor on domestic gas prices; they do not prevent 40% more liquefaction capacity from arriving and shifting negotiating power to buyers at renewal.

Financial Model: How Mispricing Becomes Numbers

Cheniere’s revenue is structurally simple: contracted volume × liquefaction fee + variable spot margin. At ~52 mtpa current capacity, with >90% under long-term contracts at $2.50–$3.00/MMBtu, base EBITDA is approximately $6.2–7.0 billion (2024 actual through 2025 guidance). EBITDA sensitivity is driven by two levers: utilization rate and renewal fee level. The company’s $8B Stage 3 + $2.9B Trains 8–9 capex assumes both levers hold at current levels through 2035+. That is the bet our thesis challenges.

Balance Sheet: Leverage Amplifies the Risk

Cheniere carried ~$22.6 billion in long-term debt at end-2024, against Consolidated Adjusted EBITDA of $6.2 billion — a net debt/EBITDA of ~3.6× (Cheniere 2024 10-K). That leverage is manageable when cash flows are stable. It amplifies losses rapidly when utilization compresses. The $8B Stage 3 plus $2.9B Trains 8–9 capex raises net debt to an estimated ~$28–30B post-expansion. In our base case where EBITDA normalizes to ~$8B and the multiple re-rates to 8.5×, enterprise value falls to ~$68B against ~$25B net debt — implying ~$43B of equity or ~$110/share. In the bear case, $7B EBITDA at 7.5× yields ~$52B EV and ~$26B of equity → ~$80–90/share. The leverage profile does not destroy equity in a normalization scenario. It is a crisis at tomorrow’s earnings — not because a catastrophe is required, but because the same leverage that makes today’s cash flows look durable amplifies every dollar of fee compression into outsized equity loss.

Long-term LNG contracts contain force majeure provisions, Material Adverse Change clauses, and destination flexibility clauses. In an oversupplied market, counterparties with declining domestic demand can reroute cargoes to the spot market and minimize contractual volumes. Contracts that look like a moat today may become the mechanism through which revenue compression arrives. They define a cash flow floor, not a ceiling.

Key Risks & Thesis Invalidation Conditions
Contract Durability Stronger Than Expected
Counterparties with take-or-pay obligations face significant financial penalties for non-performance. If energy transition economics disappoint versus current projections, customers may prefer paying fees to breaking contracts — sustaining Cheniere’s revenue floor regardless of spot market dynamics.
Thesis invalidated if: More than 80% of 2030s contracts renew at $2.25+/MMBtu; counterparty credit events remain below 2% of contracted volume through 2030.
Geopolitical LNG Demand Spike
A second major geopolitical shock — conflict in the Middle East disrupting LNG transit, Chinese blockade of Taiwan affecting Asia-Pacific gas flows, or another European gas supply crisis — could generate crisis-premium spot pricing that temporarily validates the stock’s current multiple and delays surplus realization.
Thesis invalidated if: Spot TTF prices exceed $30/MMBtu for more than 2 consecutive quarters, driving uncontracted cargo premiums that offset structural renewal pressure.
U.S. Export Policy Expansion
The Biden-era LNG export pause has been reversed. Aggressive permitting of new U.S. LNG terminals could create additional volume upside for Cheniere as the largest existing operator — though this also increases the global supply overhang long-term, sharpening the thesis eventually.
Thesis invalidated if: Cheniere secures binding SPAs for Stage 4+ capacity at $2.50+/MMBtu fee by end-2026, extending contract duration beyond the 2035 renewal risk window.
Energy Transition Slower Than Projected
Solar and wind deployment has beaten IEA forecasts for a decade. If that trend reverses — due to grid stability constraints, supply chain bottlenecks, or policy reversal in key Asian markets — LNG demand duration could extend, validating the structural demand assumptions embedded in today’s multiple.
Thesis invalidated if: IEA revises its STEPS scenario upward by >15% for 2030 LNG demand, or if Chinese coal-to-gas switching accelerates beyond current projections, sustaining global utilization above 85%.

Cheniere is an excellently run machine — a network of liquefaction trains and long-term contracts that turned America’s shale surplus into a global export business. Jack Fusco has executed against one of the most ambitious infrastructure buildouts in energy history, against the skepticism of most of the establishment, and delivered. The infrastructure is real. The contracts are real. The cash flows are real.

What is in question is the price. The investors buying at $274 are making a 20-year bet at peak-cycle economics — that today’s $2.50–$3.00/MMBtu contracted fee structure, today’s 11–12× multiple, and today’s near-zero structural surplus all persist simultaneously through a decade in which 40% more global LNG capacity arrives and every major Asian economy is aggressively building the alternatives. The contracts are only as durable as the economics that caused customers to sign them. Those customers are building, at this very moment, the infrastructure that will make renewal at today’s fees increasingly difficult to justify. Cheniere will not fail. But at $274, it doesn’t need to fail to cost you 60%. We are Short.
